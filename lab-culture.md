# Lab culture

We aim to think deeply, build carefully, learn broadly, and create a generous environment for
sharing ideas and developing as scientists. This is a living statement: lab members are encouraged
to help us revise it as the group grows.

## How we treat one another

Kindness, intellectual honesty, curiosity, and mutual respect are basic expectations. We collaborate
rather than compete with one another, share credit fairly, and make room for questions—including
questions that reveal uncertainty or challenge a senior person's view. A failed analysis or
experiment is information, not a personal failure. Problems should be discussed early so that we can
solve them together.

Academic hierarchy can make speaking candidly especially difficult in India. The PI therefore has a
responsibility to invite disagreement and listen without retaliation. Feedback should be specific,
constructive, and two-way. If a concern cannot be resolved directly, members may approach a
co-adviser, thesis committee member, the department, or an appropriate IIT Gandhinagar support
channel.


Good science benefits from people with different disciplines, regions, languages, castes, classes,
religions, genders, sexual orientations, disabilities, and neurotypes. Discrimination, harassment,
and exclusion have no place in the lab. English fluency, accent, institutional pedigree, or familiarity
with unwritten academic customs are not measures of intelligence. We will explain those customs,
make materials accessible where practicable, and support people in asking for the conditions they
need to do their best work.

## Mentoring and scientific practice

Mentoring should respond to the person's goals and stage of training. We use regular individual
meetings and lab discussions to clarify priorities, unblock work, improve writing and presentations,
and plan the next skill to learn. Expectations should be made explicit rather than left for students
to infer. Lab members should also be able to give feedback on the mentoring they receive.

We value careful reasoning over polished appearances. Wherever participant consent and privacy
allow, we document decisions, preserve reproducible analysis workflows, share materials, and use
open-science practices. Human-participant data are handled with particular care and in accordance
with ethics approvals.



## Time, wellbeing, and life outside the lab

Research is demanding, but constant availability is neither healthy nor a sign of commitment. We do
not routinely expect replies in the evening, on weekends, or on public and religious holidays. Lab
members may need flexibility for health, family and caregiving responsibilities, festivals, commuting,
or other parts of life. When workload or wellbeing becomes difficult, we encourage an early and
private conversation so that priorities, deadlines, and support can be adjusted.

Social activities are optional, inclusive, and kept affordable. No one should have to participate in
them to access mentoring, information, or professional opportunities.

## Growth, community, and responsibility

Members should leave the lab with stronger scientific judgment as well as technical skills. Subject
to available funding, we support conference participation, training, collaborations, science
communication, and preparation for careers in academia, industry, education, or public service. We
also aim to engage with schools and communities around us, and to communicate in Indian languages
when that makes our work more accessible.

We try to use research resources responsibly: plan computation before scaling it, reuse equipment,
avoid unnecessary waste, and weigh the scientific value of travel. Prospective members are welcome
to speak with current lab members about their experience before deciding whether RCP Lab is a good
fit.
